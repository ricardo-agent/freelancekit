Freelance invoice template: exactly what to include

A practical guide — 5 min read

A freelance invoice isn't complicated, but a vague or incomplete one quietly delays your money. Clients pay clear invoices faster and question messy ones. Here's exactly what every freelance invoice needs — the eight parts, in the order they should appear — plus the one field that shortens payment time more than any other.

1

The word "Invoice" + a unique number

Label it clearly and give it a number (INV-0001, INV-0002…). The number matters for your records and for the client's accounts payable system — "the invoice" is harder to chase than "invoice INV-0014."

2

Your details

Your name or business name, email, and — if you have them — address and tax/registration number. This is who the money goes to and who the client contacts with questions.

3

The client's details

Who you're billing: their business name, the contact person, and email. If they gave you a PO number, put it on — invoices with the right PO get paid without a back-and-forth.

4

Invoice date and — critically — a due date

Don't write "Net-30" and leave them to do the math. Write the actual date the money is due. This is the single field that most shortens payment time: a concrete due date creates a deadline; "Net-30" creates a vague intention.

5

Itemized work

List what you did, with quantity and rate for each line — "Homepage redesign, 12 hrs × $75" — not one lump sum. Itemized invoices look professional, are easy to approve, and pre-empt "what was this for?" questions.

6

Subtotal, tax, and total due

Show the subtotal, any tax as its own line (with your tax number if registered), and the final total in bold. The client should see the number they owe without adding anything up.

7

Payment instructions

How to pay you: bank transfer details, a payment link, or whatever you accept. The easier you make paying, the faster it happens. Missing payment details is a shockingly common reason invoices sit.

8

Terms and a late fee

State your payment terms (e.g. "Payment due within 15 days") and, if you use one, a late fee ("1.5% per month on overdue balances"). A late fee turns a soft due date into a real one.

Common questions

What's the one thing most freelancers forget? A concrete due date (a real date, not "Net-30"). It's the biggest lever on how fast you get paid.

Should I add tax? Only if you're registered for VAT/GST/sales tax — then show it as a separate line with your tax number.

What terms are fair? Net-15 or Net-30, stated as a due date, ideally with a small late fee.
